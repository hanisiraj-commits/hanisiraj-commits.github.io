We had a hypothesis that today's soft numbers are partly down to the top wagyu cuts being out of stock. I pulled 35 days of real Shopify session and conversion data to test it. One hero out barely dents blended conversion because the catalogue substitutes, but the money metric, revenue per session, drops 21% on out-of-stock days, and right now two of our three demand drivers are starved. The verdict

Directionally the hypothesis holds, with nuance. When only one hero is out and the rest of the range is stocked, blended conversion holds up (June ran only about 3% down). The dip turns real when two or more top heroes are out together, and it shows up harder in revenue per session and AOV than in the raw conversion rate. That is exactly where we sit now: the M9+ Ribeye was out for seven straight days, and the M9+ Striploin's two best cuts are still at zero.

The one caveat

Early July is also the week straight after Meat Emporium, when the deal-seekers have already bought and traffic is lower-intent. So July's drop isn't 100% down to stock. Splitting the window keeps us honest about what's stock and what's timing.

Zooming out — the full 12 months

I ran the identical test across 365 days (Jul 2025 – Jul 2026) to pressure-test it. Sale periods were separated out first, since a sale lifts conversion on its own (sale days averaged 2.2–5.3% CVR against 1.6–2.6% on normal days). The year adds one important guardrail, below.

The M9+ cuts only became genuine daily heroes in April 2026, when their combined volume jumped from around 5 units a day to 35–60. Before that they sold a couple of units a day, so a zero-sales day meant low demand, not a stock-out. That's why the proxy only reads cleanly from April on, and why the day-by-day view above sits in exactly the right window.

How we know a hero was out

Shopify can't recall historical stock levels, so I reconstructed out-of-stock days with a zero-sales proxy: a hero that normally sells every day but records zero units was almost certainly out. It lines up with what we already knew. The M9+ Ribeye and Striploin sold their last units on 29–30/06 and hit zero from 01/07, which matches what the team saw. The proxy also surfaced an earlier stock-out on 08–11 June that wasn't logged, when both M9+ cuts were out together.

Revenue at risk

Revenue per session on out-of-stock days runs $6–8 below the in-stock baseline. At around 3,400 sessions a day that's roughly $20–27k/day of exposure on a two-hero-out day, and a conservative floor that strips the post-sale effect is about $12k/day. Across the current seven-day Ribeye stock-out that's an estimated $85k–$190k of foregone revenue. Even the floor comfortably funds holding more safety stock on two SKUs.

What I'd do

01
Protect the two demand drivers first

The M9+ Ribeye and M9+ Striploin are the SKUs whose absence bends the numbers. Set a safety-stock floor so neither hits zero. The M4-5 Fillet proves a well-stocked hero holds conversion on its own.

02
Allocate at the cut level, not the product level

Stock-outs start with the popular cuts (Thin and Thick) while Half and Uncut linger, so a product can read "in stock" while its best sellers are gone. Weight replenishment toward Thin and Thick.

03
Set a low-stock trigger

Alert when a top-5 hero drops below about one to two days of cover, so purchasing can reorder before the zero-day, not after it.

04
Track revenue per session, not just CVR

Revenue per session is about 2.5× more sensitive to stock-outs than blended conversion, and it's what actually shows the lost money. Use it as the stock-out damage metric.

05
Re-run this on a clean in-stock fortnight

Once we get two weeks with all heroes stocked and no sale, I'll re-run it to strip the post-sale effect and lock a precise per-day dollar cost of a hero stock-out.
