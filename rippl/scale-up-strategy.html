Three things shaping this plan

1. Pure is a considered purchase. At ~$900 AUD with ongoing filter costs, nobody buys a reverse osmosis system on a whim. The refund data backs that up: 9 of 12 refunds over the last 180 days were Pure returns inside the 30-day guarantee, averaging $875 each. People buy it, live with it for 3–4 weeks, then decide whether to keep it. The post-purchase sequence has to work hard during that window.
2. Repeat revenue has to be built, not inherited. Pure is a one-off. Cans get reordered, but infrequently. Replacement filters run on a 6–12 month cycle. Nothing in the product mix generates natural monthly revenue the way a subscription brand does. That means repeat purchase has to be engineered through email: replenishment nudges, remineralisation education, cross-sell, and referrals. Without that layer, Rippl is an acquisition business paying to replace customers every month.
3. The list is small, and everything else depends on fixing that. Net growth of +3,546 subscribers in 180 days came mostly from one BFCM giveaway. Strip that out and organic growth is closer to ~300/month. Popup converts at 2.92% against ~22K monthly views, which is the ceiling right now. Every other recommendation here scales with list size, so this one has to work first.

Each lever targets a specific stage of the Rippl customer lifecycle. Order matters: Lever 1 feeds Lever 2, which feeds Lever 3. Lever 4 runs in parallel against the B2B audience. Running them in sequence is what closes the $60–95K/yr gap.

Grow the list 3× in 90 days

2.92% popup conversion caps everything downstream. Test a water-report hook, a quiz mechanic, and the current incentive to get to 5–7% sustained. Goal: triple the subscriber growth rate inside 90 days.

Outcome: 3× monthly signups

Route the welcome flow by what people tell us

LEVER 01

Grow the list 3× in 90 days

The welcome popup converts at 2.92% blended (1.98% desktop, 3.16% mobile). Benchmark is 5–8%. With ~22K popup views over 180 days, closing that gap alone adds 700–1,100 subscribers per month. The speed-to-first-purchase numbers below make the case pretty clearly.

78%
78% of welcome flow revenue happens on Email 1, the email that fires within the first hour of someone joining the list. 14 of 18 welcome-flow conversions ($9,738 of $11,996) come from that single email. Emails 2 and 3 add another 22% between them. New subscribers convert fast or not at all via email. So every qualified person we put on the list is effectively a short window to a potential sale. The welcome flow is already built to capitalise on that window. The constraint is how many qualified people we're feeding into it.
Current state: free mystery gift (glass Rippl bottle)
Context

The popup offers a free mystery gift, a glass Rippl bottle with any first order. No percentage discount, no price-off. That positioning fits the premium brand and avoids the discount arms race, but 2.92% conversion suggests the mechanic isn't pulling hard enough to earn the email.

Two things worth diagnosing. First, whether "mystery gift" actually reads as intriguing, or whether it just feels like another discount popup dressed up. Second, whether the glass bottle comes through visually in the popup, or whether it's abstract. Showing the actual bottle with copy like "Yours free with any order" tends to outperform the mystery framing.

Three paths to test in parallel
TestingPriority #1

Running these as parallel A/B cohorts (different traffic splits) rather than one after the other means we learn faster, and the winner surfaces in 4–6 weeks.

  1. Water report hook (recommended). "See what's actually in your tap water. Enter your postcode and we'll send you your state's water report." Seven state water-report flows already sit in the account, idle. Reactivating them as the primary lead magnet gives Rippl a hook no direct competitor has. Expected conversion: 5–8%+.
  2. Quiz mechanic. A 4–6 question quiz ("What's your water quality goal?") that collects ZPD and doubles as the capture point. The Rippl Pure Quiz form hit 69.81% conversion in its limited run. Even at 15% sustained, that's 5× the current popup. Quizzes also front-load the ZPD collection that powers Lever 2.
  3. Keep the mystery-gift incentive, tighten the creative. Show the glass bottle in the popup clearly, test "your welcome gift" vs "mystery gift" framing, rework the headline. If this beats the water report angle, we've got a winner without changing the value prop.
Water report as an exit-intent popup
TacticQuick Build

Right now nothing is funnelling visitors into the water report flow. The state flows are built, they're just idle. One clean way to drive traffic in without cannibalising the main welcome popup: run "See What's In Your Water" as an exit-intent form.

How it plays out:

  • First-visit traffic sees the standard welcome popup (mystery gift, or whichever mechanic wins the test above)
  • If they dismiss or don't convert, they browse normally
  • On exit-intent, they get the water report popup: "Before you go, see what's in your state's tap water"
  • Lower-commitment ask (no purchase context, just curiosity) with a hook nobody else can run
  • Second-chance capture rate on exit-intent popups usually runs 2–4%, which is pure upside on subscribers who would otherwise have left empty-handed

Once captured, they drop into the state-specific water report flow we already have, which doubles as the top of the welcome nurture.

Archive the 10 draft popups, keep two live
CleanupQuick Win

10 of 12 forms are sitting in draft: Rippl Giveaway, Rippl 10% First Order, New Customer $75 Pure, 2 Free Glass Bottles, Rippl Pure Discount, BF Pure 2025, and so on. Every extra popup adds overlap and muddies the visitor experience. Two live popups is the right number:

  1. Site-wide welcome popup running whichever mechanic wins the test above (refined mystery gift, water report, or quiz). Captures email plus the core ZPD fields.
  2. Exit-intent water report popup as a second-chance capture for visitors who dismissed the primary. Routes into the state water report flow.

Archive the rest. One entry popup, one exit popup.

LEVER 02

Use the zero-party data we're already collecting

The popup is already collecting ZPD (product interest, state, sometimes goals), and the welcome flow ignores all of it. Everyone gets the same generic sequence. Branching the welcome flow on stated intent is the single highest-leverage change we can make on the flow side.

Current state: one welcome flow for every intent
Problem

Today, someone signing up through the "I'm interested in Rippl Pure" popup gets the same welcome flow as someone signing up to see their state's water report. Two completely different buying intents: one is a $900 considered purchase, the other is cold curiosity. A single message doesn't serve both.

The flow's aggregate conversion at 1.92% is solid but flat across 935 recipients. With proper routing, Pure-intent subscribers should convert at 3–5% to Pure (higher AOV, higher consideration), cans-intent subscribers at 4–6% to cans (lower AOV, easier yes), and corporate leads should be nurtured toward a sample pack or booked call rather than pushed to a retail checkout.

LEVER 03

Retention and referrals, after the Pure sale

When someone buys a $900 Rippl Pure, the real work starts. The post-purchase period is when the 30-day guarantee window closes, when product habits form, and when a customer decides whether they'll stick around or disappear. It's the biggest retention lever in the account.

The Pure Owner 90-day post-purchase cadence
Core FlowRetention

Some of it is genuinely sensory (taste, clarity), some of it is expectation-driven — both are real to the customer. By Day 60 the decision feels settled. By Day 90 they're more likely to recommend it. The flow's job is reinforcement, not selling.

The upsell ladder: cans, remineralisation, replacement filters
Upsell

Once a Pure buyer is in the post-purchase cadence, there are three upsell angles in priority order:

  1. Mineral cans. "You filter water at home; what about out of the house?" Cans solve the "Rippl-quality water on the go" problem. Lower AOV, higher frequency. This is the 90-day conversion target.
  2. Remineralisation. Counter-intuitive but important. RO removes minerals, and most Pure owners don't realise it. Educational content on remineralisation ("Your RO water is perfect; here's how to add the good stuff back") positions Rippl's own mineral cans as the fix, or a mineral drops product if one launches. It's an authentic use case and a natural cross-sell.
  3. Replacement filters. 6–12 month cycle, so no immediate spike. Build the flow to fire at Month 5, Month 9, and Month 11 post-purchase with escalating reminders and an auto-ship offer. Annuity revenue.

The referral program
EvangelismRevenue Stream

Find the overlap of high-NPS and high-spend customers, then turn that group into referral partners. Everyone else stays on standard email.

Step 1 — Capture NPS via flow

Post-Purchase Day 90 email asks a single NPS question (0–10). Response writes to a custom profile property nps_score. Klaviyo handles this natively.

Step 2 — Build the evangelist segment

Segment definition: nps_score ≥ 9 AND total_order_value ≥ $900 (i.e., they bought Pure) AND days_since_last_open ≤ 30. This isolates the engaged, high-value, happy cohort. Initial size likely 5–15% of the Pure customer base.

Step 3 — Evangelist activation flow

Triggered on entering the evangelist segment. 3-email sequence:

  • Email 1: "We noticed you love Rippl. Want to share it?" (no ask yet, just recognition)
  • Email 2: The offer — $50 Amazon gift card per successful referral, plus a free glass bottle for the referred friend on their first order
  • Email 3: Ready-to-paste share templates (WhatsApp, SMS, social)

Step 4 — Track the loop

Each evangelist gets a unique Shopify discount code tied to their email. Track: codes used, revenue driven, gift cards owed. Klaviyo + Shopify handles this natively, or use a referral-specific tool (ReferralCandy, Friendbuy) if volume warrants.

Expected economics

If the cohort starts at 20–40 Pure owners and each one refers 1–2 sales in year one, that's 20–80 Pure sales from referral. At $900 AOV, that's $18K–72K in referred revenue against $1K–4K in gift card cost. Payback around 20:1. Referred customers also tend to have higher LTV (industry data puts this at ~16% higher) because they arrive already trusting the brand.

LEVER 04

Corporate customers: time email to their reorder cycle

The Corporate Can Customers list currently gets blasted 1–2× per month with generic update emails. Easy to produce, wrong shape for the audience: over-sending to low-frequency accounts (unsub risk) and under-sending to high-frequency ones (lost reorder revenue).

Current state: one cadence for every corporate account
Problem

Over the last 180 days, 7 corporate update campaigns went out ("APRIL UPDATE CORPORATE CUSTOMERS", "FEBRUARY UPDATE", "MARCH UPDATE", some resent as reminders). They perform well on engagement metrics: 92–98% opens, 0% spam, ~1–2% unsub. B2B subscribers are paying attention.

But engagement is a vanity metric here. We don't know whether these emails are actually driving reorders, and we don't know whether they're hitting each customer at the right point in their cycle. A company that reorders every 3 weeks needs a different cadence to one that reorders every 4 months.

Proposed state: per-account replenishment flow
BuildLever 04 Core

Step 1 — Study actual buying frequency

Pull 12 months of corporate orders from Shopify. For each account, calculate:

  • Average days between orders (their "replenishment cycle")
  • Average order value
  • Typical product mix (flavours, sizes)

This surfaces natural clusters. Some accounts probably land at 2–3 weeks (high-traffic offices, event spaces), some at 4–6 weeks (mid-size offices), some at 8–12 weeks (seasonal use). Data-driven rather than guessed.

Step 2 — Store the cycle as a profile property

Write each account's replenishment_cycle_days to their Klaviyo profile via Shopify sync or one-time CSV import. This becomes the fuel for Step 3.

Step 3 — Build the per-account replenishment flow

Single flow triggered on "Placed Order where customer is tagged Corporate". Flow waits for the profile's replenishment_cycle_days value minus 5 days (reminder arrives before they run out). First email: friendly "Time to reorder?" with a one-click reorder link pre-filled with their last order's contents.

Every expansion from here is a deliberate warming step, not a return to pre-BFCM send volume.

Apply exclusion segments to every send (Recently Purchased L7, Received 3+ Emails L24h, Bounce L30, Suppressed). They're already built; they just need to actually get applied to each campaign.

Content calendar: 50/50 promo and content
Planning

Target 8–10 campaigns per month at steady state. Rough mix:

  • 4 content emails per month — seasonal or state-specific water quality reports, remineralisation education, B2B case studies, UGC roundups, recipe and pairing content, founder plain-text
  • 4 promo emails per month — product launches, bundle offers, seasonal sales (never same-day doubles, always with exclusion segments applied)
  • 1–2 operational — corporate updates, new state availability, shipping cutoffs

Content emails keep the list warm between promos. The World Water Day email hit 94% open and 2.11% CTR, which shows what's possible when content is genuinely useful. Doing that four times a month builds engagement signal and protects deliverability on promo sends.

A/B testing: start with subject lines, expand from there
Testing

Zero A/B tests are running currently. Klaviyo has native A/B testing on every send, so there's no reason not to start.

  • Month 1: subject line A/B on every send (10% holdout each variant, 80% to winner)
  • Month 2: sender name tests ("Rippl" vs "Rippl Team" vs "[Founder name]")
  • Month 3: send time tests (morning vs evening, weekday vs weekend)

Document winners monthly. Winning patterns feed back into template defaults. Expect a 5–15% open rate lift inside 90 days from subject-line testing alone, and that compounds across every flow and campaign going forward.
