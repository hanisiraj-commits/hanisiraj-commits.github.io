Scale-Up Strategy · April 2026

Build the list. Activate the data. Turn customers into evangelists.

A four-lever plan to scale Rippl's email program from today's ~$45K/yr to $105–140K/yr in 90 days — without adding discounting pressure, without new paid tools required, and without compromising the deliverability recovery already underway.

Rippl is a premium hydration brand with unicorn-level social proof (650+ B2B brands including L'Oréal, Mercedes-Benz, New Balance) selling a one-time big-ticket product (Rippl Pure, ~$900) plus a recurring consumable (330ml mineral cans). The business model demands a retention-led email strategy — the revenue is not in winning first-time buyers over and over, it's in converting cold traffic into Pure buyers with conviction, then building a second income stream off those buyers through cans, remineralization education, replacement filters, and referrals. This doc lays out exactly how.

Three truths we're planning around

Truth 1 — Rippl Pure is a conviction sale, not a spontaneous buy. At ~$900 AUD with ongoing filter replacement cost, no one impulse-buys a reverse osmosis system online. The data backs this up: 9 of 12 refunds over 180 days were Pure returns under the 30-day guarantee, averaging $875 each. People buy it, live with it for 3–4 weeks, then decide whether to keep it. Every post-purchase email is either reinforcing the decision to keep it or losing the sale. This isn't a funnel. It's a conviction journey.
Truth 2 — The recurring revenue isn't organic, it has to be engineered. Rippl Pure is one-off. Cans are consumable but bought infrequently at retail prices. Replacement filters are a 6–12 month cycle. None of this generates natural monthly revenue the way a subscription brand does. The brand either builds repeat purchase muscle through email — replenishment nudges, remineralization education, cross-sell ladders, referrals — or it's a pure acquisition business paying to replace customers each month.
Truth 3 — The list is small. All other levers follow from fixing that. Net growth of +3,546 subscribers in 180 days is mostly from one BFCM giveaway. Without that spike, organic growth is ~300/month. At a 2.92% popup conversion against ~22K monthly popup views, the ceiling is visible. Every other strategic recommendation in this document compounds off list size. Get list growth wrong and everything else runs into a wall.

LEVER 01

Grow the list — 3× in 90 days

The welcome popup converts at 2.92% blended (1.98% desktop, 3.16% mobile). Benchmark is 5–8%. With ~22K popup views in 180 days, closing that gap alone adds 700–1,100 subscribers per month. And the speed-to-first-purchase data below makes the business case for list growth almost self-proving.

78%
78% of welcome flow revenue happens on Email 1 — the email that fires within the first hour of someone joining the list. 14 of 18 welcome-flow conversions ($9,738 of $11,996) come from that single email. Email 2 and 3 add another 22% combined. This is the strongest argument for aggressive list growth you'll see: new subscribers don't hibernate — they convert almost immediately, or not at all via email. Every extra person on the list who fits Rippl's profile is a ~1-hour window to a potential sale. The welcome flow is already built to capitalize. The only constraint is how many qualified people we're putting in front of it.
The current state: free mystery gift (glass Rippl bottle)
Context

The current popup offers a free mystery gift — a glass Rippl bottle with any first order. No percentage discount, no price-off. That positioning is aligned with the premium brand (no discount arms race) but the 2.92% conversion suggests the mechanic isn't landing strongly enough to overcome the friction of handing over an email.

Two diagnostic questions worth testing: (1) Is the mystery framing actually intriguing, or does "mystery gift" feel like another discount popup dressed up? (2) Does the glass bottle visually come through in the popup, or is it abstract? Visual specificity usually beats abstraction — a clear photo of the bottle + "Yours free with any order" often outperforms the mystery framing.

Three paths to test in parallel
TestingPriority #1

Running these as parallel A/B cohorts (different traffic splits) rather than sequentially means we learn faster and the winner emerges within 4–6 weeks.

  1. The water report hook (recommended test) — "See what's actually in your tap water. Enter your postcode and we'll send you your state's water report." Seven state water-report flows already exist in the account. They're currently idle and under-utilized. Reactivating them as the primary lead magnet gives Rippl a hook no competitor can copy. Expected conversion: 5–8%+.
  2. The quiz mechanic — a 4–6 question quiz ("What's your water quality goal?") that collects ZPD AND functions as the capture point. The Rippl Pure Quiz form hit 69.81% conversion in its limited run. Even at 15% sustained, it's 5× current popup performance. Quizzes also front-load the ZPD collection that powers Lever 2.
  3. Keep the mystery-gift incentive, refine the creative — show the glass bottle in the popup explicitly, test "your welcome gift" vs "mystery gift" framing, tighten the headline. If this beats the water report angle, we've got our winner without leaving the current value prop.
The water report as exit-intent specifically
TacticQuick Build

Right now we're not funneling visitors into the water report flow at all — it sits idle even though the state flows are built. One clean way to drive traffic into it without cannibalizing the main welcome popup: run "See What's In Your Water" as an exit-intent form.

The logic:

  • First-visit visitors see the standard welcome popup (mystery gift or whichever winner from test above)
  • If they dismiss or don't convert, they browse
  • On exit-intent, they get the water report popup: "Before you go — see what's in your state's tap water"
  • It's a lower-commitment ask (no purchase context, just curiosity) with a unique hook
  • Second-chance capture rate on exit-intent popups typically runs 2–4% — pure upside on subscribers who would have left empty-handed

Once captured, they land in the state-specific water report flow we already have built, which doubles as the top of the welcome nurture. Two birds.

Kill the 10 draft popups · activate exactly two
CleanupQuick Win

10 of 12 forms are sitting in draft: Rippl Giveaway, Rippl 10% First Order, New Customer $75 Pure, 2 Free Glass Bottles, Rippl Pure Discount, BF Pure 2025, etc. More popups = more overlap = worse user experience. Rippl needs exactly two live popups:

  1. Site-wide welcome popup — whichever mechanic wins the test above (mystery gift refined / water report / quiz). Collects email + core ZPD fields.
  2. Exit-intent water report popup — second-chance capture for visitors who dismissed the primary. Routes into the state water report flow.

Archive every other draft. One entry popup, one exit popup. Nothing else.

LEVER 02

Activate the zero-party data we're already collecting

The popup collects ZPD (product interest, state, sometimes goals) but the welcome flow ignores it — everyone gets the same generic sequence. Routing subscribers down different welcome tracks based on their stated interest is the single highest-leverage change we can make on the flow side.

Current state: one-size-fits-all welcome
Problem

Today, someone who signs up via the "I'm interested in Rippl Pure" popup gets the same welcome flow as someone who signs up to see their state's water report. These are two completely different buying intents — one is a $900 considered purchase, the other is cold curiosity. One message can't serve both.

The flow's aggregate conversion at 1.92% is solid but flat across 935 recipients. With proper routing, Pure-intent subscribers should convert at 3–5% to Pure (higher AOV, higher consideration), cans-intent subscribers should convert at 4–6% to cans (lower AOV, easier yes), and corporate leads should be nurtured toward a sample pack or booked call rather than pushed to a retail checkout.

LEVER 03

Retention, upsell & evangelism — after the Pure sale

When someone buys a $900 Rippl Pure, we don't stop marketing to them — we start. The post-purchase period is when buyer's remorse happens (the 30-day guarantee window), when product habits form, and when a customer decides if they're an advocate or a one-and-done. This is the single biggest retention lever in the account.

The upsell ladder — cans, remineralization, replacement filters
Upsell

Once a Pure buyer is in the conviction cadence, there are three upsell angles in priority order:

  1. Mineral cans (the immediate win) — "You filter water at home. What about out of the house?" Cans solve the "Rippl-quality water on the go" problem. Low AOV, higher frequency. This is the 90-day conversion target.
  2. Remineralization (the education play) — counter-intuitive but important: RO removes minerals. Most Pure owners don't know this. Educational campaigns on remineralization ("Your RO water is perfect — here's how to add the good stuff back") position Rippl's own mineral cans as the solution, or sell a mineral drops product if launched. Irony aside, it's an authentic use case and a natural cross-sell.
  3. Replacement filters (the long game) — 6–12 month cycle. Can't drive immediate spikes. Build the flow so it fires at Month 5, Month 9, Month 11 post-purchase with escalating reminders + auto-ship offer. Annuity revenue, not a short-term win.

The referral program — NPS intersection, tangible incentives
EvangelismRevenue Stream

Identify the intersection of high-NPS × high-spend customers, turn them into referral partners. Everyone else gets standard email. The evangelist cohort gets special attention.

Step 1 — Capture NPS via flow

Post-Purchase Day 90 email asks a single NPS question (0–10). Response writes to a custom profile property nps_score. Klaviyo handles this natively.

Step 2 — Build the evangelist segment

Segment definition: nps_score ≥ 9 AND total_order_value ≥ $900 (i.e., they bought Pure) AND days_since_last_open ≤ 30. This isolates the engaged, high-value, happy cohort. Initial size likely 5–15% of the Pure customer base.

Step 3 — Evangelist activation flow

Triggered on entering the evangelist segment. 3-email sequence:

  • Email 1: "We noticed you love Rippl — want to share it?" (no ask yet, just recognition)
  • Email 2: The referral offer — $50 Amazon gift card for every successful referral; referred friend gets a free glass bottle with their first order
  • Email 3: Ready-to-paste share templates (WhatsApp / SMS / social)

Step 4 — Track the loop

Each evangelist gets a unique Shopify discount code tied to their email. Track: codes used, revenue driven, gift cards owed. Klaviyo + Shopify handles this natively, or use a referral-specific tool (ReferralCandy, Friendbuy) if volume warrants.

Expected economics

If the evangelist cohort starts at 20–40 Pure owners and each refers even 1–2 sales in the first year, that's 20–80 Pure sales from referral = $18K–72K in referred revenue at $900 AOV, with $1K–4K in gift card cost. Payback ~20:1. Referred customers also have materially higher LTV (industry data: ~16% higher) because they enter already trusting the brand.

LEVER 04

Corporate customers — personalize the replenishment

The Corporate Can Customers list is currently blasted 1–2× per month with generic update emails. Efficient to produce, suboptimal in impact — over-sending to low-frequency accounts (unsub risk) and under-sending to high-frequency accounts (lost reorder revenue).

Current state: spray-and-pray to B2B
Problem

Over the last 180 days, 7 corporate update campaigns went out ("APRIL UPDATE CORPORATE CUSTOMERS", "FEBRUARY UPDATE", "MARCH UPDATE" — some sent twice as reminders). They perform well on engagement metrics: 92–98% open rates, 0% spam, ~1–2% unsub rate. B2B subscribers are paying attention.

But engagement is vanity here — we don't know if these emails are driving reorders, and we don't know if we're hitting each customer at the right point in their buying cycle. A company that reorders every 3 weeks needs different cadence than one that reorders every 4 months.

Proposed state: per-account personalized replenishment
BuildLever 04 Core

Step 1 — Study actual buying frequency

Pull 12 months of corporate orders from Shopify. For each account, calculate:

  • Average days between orders (their "replenishment cycle")
  • Average order value
  • Typical product mix (flavours, sizes)

This surfaces natural clusters — likely some accounts at 2–3 weeks (high-traffic offices, event spaces), some at 4–6 weeks (mid-size offices), some at 8–12 weeks (seasonal use). Data-driven, not guessed.

Step 2 — Store the cycle as a profile property

Write each account's replenishment_cycle_days to their Klaviyo profile via Shopify sync or one-time CSV import. This becomes the fuel for Step 3.

Step 3 — Build the per-account replenishment flow

Single flow triggered on "Placed Order where customer is tagged Corporate". Flow waits for the profile's replenishment_cycle_days value minus 5 days (reminder arrives before they run out). First email: friendly "Time to reorder?" with a one-click reorder link pre-filled with their last order's contents.

Content calendar — 50/50 promo/content mix
Planning

Target 8–10 campaigns per month at steady state. Rough mix:

  • 4 content emails/month — water quality reports (seasonal/state-specific), remineralization education, B2B case studies, UGC roundups, recipe/pairing content, founder plain-text
  • 4 promo emails/month — product launches, bundle offers, seasonal sales (never same-day doubles, always with exclusion segments)
  • 1–2 operational — corporate updates, new state availability, shipping cutoffs

Content emails keep the list warm between promos — the World Water Day email at 94% open / 2.11% CTR shows what's possible when content is genuinely useful. Replicating that 4× per month compounds engagement signal and protects deliverability on promo sends.

A/B testing — start with subject lines, expand from there
Testing

Zero A/B tests currently running. Klaviyo has native A/B testing on every send — it costs nothing to start.

  • Month 1: subject line A/B on every send (10% holdout each variant, 80% to winner)
  • Month 2: sender name tests ("Rippl" vs "Rippl Team" vs "[Founder name]")
  • Month 3: send time tests (morning vs evening, weekday vs weekend)

Document winners monthly. Winning patterns feed back into template defaults. Expect 5–15% open rate lift within 90 days from subject-line testing alone — and that compounds across every flow and campaign going forward.
