Spend & Save + Gift With Purchase · BAU Test

Daily deals: what happened, what we learned, and how we run them next.

Two BAU flash tests — a tiered Spend & Save (12–15 Jun) and a tiered Gift With Purchase (19 Jun). The Spend & Save moved revenue but the economics were thin and US-only; the GWP came in weak on every leading indicator. Both point to the same fixes. Here's the read on each, the shared insights, and the playbook for next time.

Section 1

What happened

Each deal read against matched BAU weekdays. Spend & Save has a full window; the GWP is an early read — firm on its send metrics and gift-tier uptake, preliminary on day-level revenue (it launched 19 Jun 12am Perth, so only the first part of day one was complete at pull).

01 · Spend & Save 12–15 Jun · target $342k / 72h · email + SMS + on-site

Launched broad to 428k on 12 Jun, paused within a day after the founder flagged it was more confusing than compelling, then re-cut as a targeted US email + SMS on 14–15 Jun. The targeted version is the only part that moved the number.

The mechanic works: red-light units roughly doubled (217 → 456), attach SKUs doubled, basket depth rose +18% — real basket-building, not just discounting demand that existed anyway. But the economics are thin: a +$82k gross lift minus $7.2k pull-forward nets ~$74.8k, and $42.2k of it was handed back as discount. After discount and COGS, two-day contribution lands near $22–40k — before paid, affiliate disruption and team time take it close to a wash.

Confound: June paid pushed to its $287.5k ceiling and the lift skewed to new customers (146/176 vs ~95 BAU) — some uplift was paid-acquired. Contribution range illustrative at 55–70% full-price hardware margin.

  • Broad email flopped: 428k recipients → $13,703 at $0.03 per recipient — the same as a normal content email, no "sale" lift.
  • Only the targeted US sends lifted basket: US email AOV $567, SMS AOV $601 (2.3× the $258 broad AOV) — the mechanic needs a high-intent audience.
  • Codes were switched off during the promo, breaking the affiliate channel mid-weekend — influencers' codes stopped working and the base lost its usual saving.
  • The cart broke the core motion: the Rebuy slide-cart froze, blocking the "add more to hit the next tier" behaviour the deal depends on.
  • Demand was pulled forward, not created: 16 Jun conversion crashed to 0.74% (month's worst), erasing $7.2k of the lift.

The email program that carried the tests

  • Over-mailing eroded the open rate: seven full-file blasts in nine days (two on 13 Jun) pulled opens from 71% on content down to 43% on the GWP — the deal that needed reach most landed on the most fatigued list.
  • No real segmentation on the deals: both went to the whole database (428k / 424k). The "USA" send was still 323k — a geo-filter, not intent — and carried the highest unsub (0.18%) and spam (0.028%, ~5× the others).
  • One-shot blasts, not sequences: each 72-hour window got ~1 email + 1 SMS. No teaser, midpoint reminder or final-hours push, so the urgency-driven last-day revenue a flash sale relies on never fired.
  • The channel mix was inverted: SMS converted at $0.25/recipient (~8× email) but got tiny lists (29k, then 9k) and a single send; email, at $0.03, carried the load at full-file scale.
  • The calendar wasn't deconflicted: the BC Method launch (Tease 10th, Launch 17th, Panel 20th) straddled both deals — the GWP landed one day after the BC Method Launch, two big events back-to-back to the same file.
  • The angle was wrong for the moment: into a just-bought, discount-fatigued list, the content emails out-earned the deals — UGC $11,926 and Global Wellness $9,597 vs the GWP's $4,552.
  • The list-health bill is mounting: repeated full-file blasts pushed unsubs to 0.10–0.20% and SMS opt-outs to 0.8–0.9%; the GWP's depressed 43% open is partly the deliverability tax of the week before it.
The CRM fix, in five moves

Sequence every deal (teaser → launch → midpoint → final hours) · segment to intent not geography, reserving the full file for one launch moment · lead with SMS and cap full-file email frequency · suppress recent purchasers and the unengaged · deconflict the calendar so only one event owns the inbox at a time.

Insights — what the two tests taught us

Both deals failed in the same places, which is the useful part: the lessons generalise to any daily deal we run.

1

Perceived value is the ceiling — in BAU a deal competes with our own discounting

Both

Bon Charge runs 15% welcome + 25–38% blended affiliate/podcast discounting every day. Both deals offered the same or less, so most customers had no reason to act. A daily deal only works when it beats what's already available.

2

Thresholds have to match the real basket curve

Both

Median order is $205 (and $349 on the GWP send day) — the GWP entry was set $1 above it, so 68% qualified for nothing. Only 6% of orders reach the $1,000 tier, a figure that doesn't move even in a peak sale. Tiers set above the basket lock most customers out.

3

The mechanic does change behaviour — when it's reachable and targeted

Spend & Save

On the targeted US audience, units doubled, basket depth rose +18% and AOV hit $567–601. Spend-and-save genuinely drives bundling and high-ticket attach. The problem was never the idea — it was the economics and the reach.

4

A gift is weaker than cash — and gifting sellable SKUs costs margin twice

GWP

At $350 the $20 sticker is worth 5.7% of spend vs the 15–25% a code already gives; the gift only beats a 25% code at $1,000+ (12% of orders). Worse, gifting the $149 wand and $199 Bullet cannibalises products we sell. Make gifts genuinely desirable and non-sellable, or use cash-equivalents.

5

SMS converts, email reaches — and the list needs protecting

Both

SMS drove ~8× the revenue per recipient of email. But five 350k+ blasts in ten days pushed the GWP open rate to 37%, and crowding it against a product launch split attention further. Lead with SMS, space the email, isolate the deal.

6

Timing and isolation decide the read

Both

Running 12 days after an L1, on a weekend, into a fatigued just-bought list, suppressed response and pulled demand forward (the 0.74% trough). Daily deals need a fresh audience reason and a clean window — and must be judged net of pull-forward against a matched baseline.

7

Execution is a value multiplier

Both

A frozen cart, disabled codes, USD-shaped thresholds and an unbriefed weekend team each leaked value before the offer could land. The deal mechanic is only as good as the build, the QA and the team prep around it.

Section 4

Going forward — the daily-deals playbook

The tests didn't disprove daily deals — they showed the conditions a daily deal needs to earn its margin. Here's how we run them from here, built straight from the seven insights.

Play 01Stack on a sale, don't compete in BAU

Daily deals belong inside the L1, layered on the sitewide offer.

In BAU the deal fought our own codes and netted just $1.77 per $1 of discount. On top of a 25% sitewide sale, an extra tier is genuinely incremental and the customer can't get it elsewhere — which is the original thesis worth proving at Mid-Year.

Play 02Offer has to beat the live code

If a welcome or affiliate code is worth more, the deal won't move.

Either make the tiered saving clearly larger than every live code, or run the deal where codes are suppressed. Never ship a deal smaller than what's already in the customer's pocket.

Play 03Calibrate thresholds to the basket

Entry near the median ($205), tiers set per currency.

Build each market's ladder off its own AUD/GBP/EUR order distribution, not converted USD. Re-run the order-value percentiles before locking, so the majority can unlock something and the top tier is actually reachable.

Play 04Pick the right mechanic

Favour reachable, high-perceived-value mechanics.

Category / hero-product spot sales, build-your-own-bundle, and cash-back or gift-card incentives tend to beat a low-value GWP. Reserve GWP for genuinely desirable, non-sellable gifts at thresholds most baskets can hit.

Play 05SMS-led, email for reach, isolated

Lead the high-intent push with SMS; protect the email list.

Use SMS for conversion (~8× efficiency) and email for reach, space the mass sends, and keep the deal clear of product-launch windows so it isn't fighting for the inbox — the mistake that hit the GWP.

Play 06Clean window, full pre-flight

Weekday launch, team briefed, build QA'd, away from the L1 afterglow.

Run the pre-flight checklist: brief CS, affiliates and influencers; confirm code behaviour; QA the cart, tier mechanic and per-currency thresholds. Don't launch a discount straight after a major sale into a just-bought list.

Play 07Measure on contribution, not revenue

Judge every deal net of discount and pull-forward, against a margin floor.

Read incrementality versus a matched baseline, subtract the next-day trough and the extra discount, and decide go/no-go on contribution — not the raw day. Set a minimum net-per-$1-discount the deal must clear to repeat.

The Mid-Year test

The five daily-deal windows already mapped for 9–26 Jul are the right place to prove incrementality — stacked on the sale, SMS-led, thresholds recalibrated per currency, and gifts or cash-back in place of stickers. Run them clean and we'll finally answer the brand's question with a number, not a hunch.
