Spend & Save + Gift With Purchase · June 2026

Daily deals: what happened, why they underperformed, and how we run them next.

Two flash tests this month — a tiered Spend & Save (12–15 Jun) and a tiered Gift With Purchase (18–19 Jun). Neither moved the number the way it needed to. Reading the order, discount and send data together, they fell short for the same handful of reasons — and every one is fixable. Here's the plain read, backed by the numbers.

Section 1

What happened

Each deal is read against a normal (BAU) trading day — a typical day does $61k net on 214 orders at a 29% discount. The question for each: did the deal day beat that, and what did it cost in discount? One modestly beat a normal day on its targeted audience but washed out over the full window; the other didn't beat a normal day at all.

On the targeted US audience (14–15 Jun) the deal did beat a normal day — net revenue +27% with deeper baskets (1.80 vs 1.61 items) — and, importantly, it cost no more discount than an ordinary day (27% vs 29%). But the broad launch on the 12th flopped and was paused within a day, and a next-day demand dip followed, so across the full 4-day window the gain washed out to roughly flat (+4%). Execution leaked value too — affiliate codes broke mid-weekend and the cart froze.

The GWP showed no lift over a normal day — the 18th landed within 1% of a typical day and the 19th send day was tracking below (only partly complete at pull) — yet it cost the same ~30% of order value in discount and gifts. The reason: the median order was just ~$200, well below the $350 entry, so 76% unlocked nothing; three-quarters of those who did still stacked a ~20% code, leaving the $20 sticker meaningless (fewer than 50 gifts claimed all weekend). The messaging was clear and the build clean; the miss was the offer structure.

Two things stand out across both deals. First, neither was "bought" with extra markdown — the discount given (27–30% of order value) was no deeper than a normal day's 29%. Second, cart abandonment held steady at ~15% (same as BAU), so people weren't dropping carts at the post-discount price. The shortfall was a demand problem — not over-discounting or checkout friction. And even the best deal days came nowhere near a true Bon Charge big day ($250k+ gross): these traded like a slightly-good normal day, not a sale.

Why it didn't work — the data

Both deals failed in the same places. Pulling normal trading, the order ledger and the send logs together, six reasons explain it — and the first one is the root of most.

1

The offer never beat the discount customers already get

Both

Bon Charge gives away ~30% of gross revenue in discounts every day, and 70% of orders take a discount without even entering a code (automatic offers averaging ~30% off). On top of that sit standing codes at 15% (affiliate/podcast) and 25–30% (cart, VIP, win-back). A Spend & Save worth ~20% or a $20 gift simply couldn't beat what was already in the customer's pocket. A deal only works when it's clearly better than the everyday price.

2

The threshold sat above where people actually buy

Both

At order level the median GWP order was just ~$200 (~$254 in the US) — the $350 US entry sat well above where most people buy. The result: 76% of orders unlocked no tier at all, and only ~8% reached the $1,000 tier. When the entry is set above the basket curve, most customers are locked out before the offer can work.

3

Even stacked with a code, the reward didn't beat the everyday discount

GWP

The entry gift — a $20 sticker on a $350 order — is just 5.7%. Customers could stack a 15% code on top, and three-quarters of GWP orders did (median ~20% off) — so the sticker simply landed on top of a discount that already beat it, still short of the 25–30% a price-sensitive slice of the base gets routinely. For the customers most likely to chase a deal, the gift added nothing they couldn't already get — and fewer than 50 gifts were claimed across both days. Higher up the ladder it gets worse: a $149 wand or $199 Bullet given away is a sellable SKU lost.

4

The tiers were built around the US, not each market

Both

The entry was set at $300–350 for the US; other regions got higher, variable thresholds that weren't calibrated to their local baskets. So how reachable the deal was swung market to market — sized where it was designed (the US, ~two-thirds of orders) and inconsistent everywhere else. For a store selling in 90+ currencies, every market needs its own ladder, built off its own order curve.

5

One global email, delivered at 2am for Australia

Both

Both deals went as a single worldwide blast (no AU- or US-specific send), timed to 9am US Pacific — which is midnight Perth = 2am on the AU east coast. Australia is the #4 region (9% of orders) but only 5% of AU orders ever happen at that hour. The email landed while AU slept, then sat behind a day of other sends. Region-split, local-time sends would have fixed this for free.

6

The lift came from new customers, not bigger baskets

Both

66–68% of deal orders were new customers (vs 64% in normal trading) — people who'd convert on a welcome code anyway — while average order value barely moved. The deal mostly re-papered demand that already existed rather than creating incremental, behaviour-changing spend.

Section 3

Going forward — how we run daily deals

The tests didn't disprove daily deals — they showed the conditions a deal needs to earn its margin. Five changes, built straight from the data above.

Play 01Beat the everyday price, or stack on a sale

Never ship a deal smaller than what's already available.

Either make the saving clearly larger than every live code, or run it where codes are suppressed, or layer it on top of an L1 sale so the extra tier is genuinely incremental. In normal trading the deal just fought our own ~30% discounting.

Play 02Calibrate thresholds per currency, near the median

Set the entry close to each market's own median order, not a number borrowed from the US.

Build the AU/US/UK/EU ladders off each region's own order distribution before locking, so the majority can unlock something and the top tier is actually reachable.

Play 03Test simple before complex

Try a single aggressive discount on a hero product first.

The Red Light Face Mask alone did 871 units / $230k in 19 days — ~3× the next product. A blunt, clear spot-deal on a bestseller is easier to grasp, build and QA than a three-tier dollar-value ladder, and gives a clean read on whether a daily deal moves the number at all.

Play 04Region-split, local-time, SMS-led

Send AU in AEST, US in PT, UK in GMT — and lead with SMS.

Split the audience by market and schedule each in local morning/evening instead of one global blast. Lead the high-intent push with SMS (far higher revenue per recipient), use email for reach, and keep the deal clear of product-launch windows.

Play 05Judge on contribution, not revenue

Read every deal net of discount and pull-forward, against a margin floor.

Measure incrementality versus a matched baseline, subtract the extra discount and the next-day trough, and set a minimum net-per-$1-of-discount the deal must clear to repeat. Pre-flight the build, codes, cart and team brief every time.

The bottom line

Daily deals can work for Bon Charge — but only when the offer beats the everyday discount, the thresholds match each market's real basket, and the send reaches every region in its own local time. Fix those three and the Mid-Year windows (9–26 Jul) can finally answer the question with a number, not a hunch.
