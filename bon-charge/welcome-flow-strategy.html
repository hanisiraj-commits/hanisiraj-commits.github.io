The Day 1 email isn't selling. It's making the science real.

Sarah didn't buy yesterday. The new flow doesn't push harder. It gets calmer. Instead of another offer, she gets the mechanism — why red light actually works, shown as a cross-section diagram of her skin with the light reaching the dermis layer where her serums can't go.

Subject
"How red light reaches what skincare can't"
Job to be done
Educate · build intellectual trust

What Sarah experiences

The hero is calmer. A single image, a confident headline ("How red light reaches what skincare can't"), and a sub-headline that quietly reframes how she's been thinking about skin products. The 15% code sits underneath, but as supporting text, not the main act.

Underneath, the centrepiece: a cross-section diagram of skin layers, with Epidermis on top (where skincare lives) and Dermis underneath (where collagen and fibroblasts live). Red light reaches the lower layer. A paragraph would have to explain this. The diagram just shows it.

Then an FAQ block — three questions a sceptical first-time buyer would actually ask. How long until results. Will it replace her routine. How often. Answers stay brief and honest, and stay inside TGA-aligned language ("experiences may vary", "designed to support what you already do").

Why this email earns its place

The customer who didn't buy on Day 0 needs a different reason to open on Day 1. Push the offer harder, you lose trust. Teach her something, you build it. The diagram and FAQ together answer her objections before she has to voice them. Each pillar gets its own version of this email: Hair → "The hair routine you can actually wear." Recovery → "Most bodies recover at half-speed." Sleep → "Most sleep problems start three hours before bed."

The last personalised send. Tight, deliberate, every friction stripped out.

By Day 3 the brand has earned the right to put specific products in front of her. E3 looks visually different from the first two emails. A free-shipping and 12-month-warranty banner up top, then a chunky code-reveal block (not a thin strip), then an editorial product trio still life, then the three Face products with more detail on each one.

Subject
"You don't need a complicated skin routine"
Job to be done
Convert via simplicity + stat-backed certainty

What Sarah experiences

The shipping and warranty banner does work before she's even seen a product. It tells her the transaction is low-risk. Free shipping. Twelve-month warranty. The code block underneath is intentionally bigger than a sticker. The offer is treated as a section in its own right, not a footnote at the top.

The three product cards now use "Pick this if:" framing. Short, declarative lines matching a concern to a product — full-face, neck-and-chest, or targeted. Underneath each card a stat callout: "98% reported more even-looking tone." Real numbers from Ana's n=50 review analysis. Nothing made up.

Closes on a secondary "TAKE THE WELLNESS FINDER" CTA. For the customer who's still not sure, the quiz is the soft landing.

Why this email earns its place

This is the final send in the high-intent window. Every module is taking a different kind of risk off the table. The banner takes off financial risk. The "Pick this if:" lines take off decision risk. The stat callouts take off outcome risk. The Wellness Finder takes off choice paralysis. If Sarah is going to buy from the personalised arm of the flow, E3 is the email that closes her. After this, the flow widens — past Day 3 the data says pushing one ZPD signal further costs more than it earns.

02 — Tactics
01

VIP Early Access — give the warm list a reason to opt in.

Existing + new subscribers · additional discount layered on top of the public sale

The highest-leverage move in the lead-up week is converting the warm list into a self-identified buyer cohort. Open a VIP early-access opt-in form (Klaviyo form + dedicated landing page) that promises an extra discount on top of the Memorial Day sale, in exchange for the subscriber telling us they're in. A one-email confirmation flow fires immediately with the code, a minimum order value, and multi-use enabled.

The real value is in the segment we build out of it. Every opt-in is now a high-intent CLC segment we can use for the rest of the year. Lift on a VIP code over a standard public sale code typically runs 1.4–1.8× on CTR and 1.2–1.5× on purchase rate.

02

Daily stackable deals — a fresh reason to open every day.

Three sub-mechanics rotated through the seven-day window

A single sitewide discount runs hot on day one and fades by day three. The fix: a daily-deals layer that rotates a new, time-bound mechanic on top of the base sale. Subscribers get a reason to come back, and list fatigue turns into list engagement.

Mechanic A — Spend & Save

TieredTier-based discount that scales with cart value. Lifts AOV +15–25% on tier days.

Mechanic B — Boosted Welcome

Temp liftWelcome offer temporarily increased for non-purchasers. Lifts new-buyer conversion +30–45%.

Mechanic C — Hero Flash

Single SKUDeeper discount on one hero product per day. Creates a "today only" reason to look.

Why these compound rather than cannibalise. Each mechanic targets a different motivation — Spend & Save for the bundler, Boosted Welcome for the unconverted, Hero Flash for the comparison shopper.
